Cold-Weather Firecraft & Fatwood Chemistry

Fatwood chemistry, snow platforms, and ignition drills for when everything is cold and wet.

1 | Fire in the Cold: The Physics Problem

In sub-freezing air, two enemies fight ignition: conduction and phase change. Cold metal, snow, and damp wood absorb ignition heat faster than you can generate it. The solution is insulation — a vapor barrier between your flame and the frozen world.

  • Platform first: Create a dry base of split sticks or bark. Snow steals BTUs by melting and refreezing beneath your tinder.
  • Reflective shield: Angle green logs or a Mylar blanket behind the fire to rebound radiant heat toward you and the coals.
  • Fuel prep ratio: You’ll need roughly 3× more tinder and 2× more kindling than in summer conditions to overcome cold inertia.

2 | Fatwood Chemistry 101

Fatwood is the resin-rich heartwood found at the base of pine stumps. When microbes die back after the tree falls, Pinus resinosa channels resin toward the wound site, saturating the wood with terpenes such as abietic acid and pinene. These compounds vaporize at ≈ 150 °C and ignite near 230 °C — far lower than cellulose (≈ 300 °C).

  • Density test: A good piece sinks halfway in water — resin adds mass and hydrophobicity.
  • Color cue: Amber to dark orange grain = highest terpene content.
  • Odor check: Sharp turpentine smell indicates fresh volatiles; muted scent = oxidized, slower burn.

3 | Extraction & Prep

Split the root base of a fallen pine or the knot cluster where branches met the trunk. Shave curls using the spine of your knife or a ferro-rod scraper. Each shaving exposes volatile oils; fine dust mixed with curls produces a two-stage ignition system: vapor flash followed by sustained resin burn.

Tip: Keep fatwood sealed in a waxed paper pouch — not plastic — so it can off-gas moisture without losing volatility.

4 | Biology of Combustion

Combustion is simply oxidative respiration accelerated by heat. The same O₂ → CO₂ conversion that powers mitochondria also drives fire. Terpenes act as “lipid calories” for your flame, yielding about 40 MJ / kg — roughly the energy density of diesel. That’s why a 2-ounce chunk of fatwood can boil 500 ml of water in ≈ 6 minutes under a windscreen.

5 | Field Drill: The Snow Platform Burn

  1. Lay a 1 × 1 ft platform of split sticks atop packed snow (at least 3 cm thick).
  2. Feather two sticks of fatwood into curls; arrange in a cross pattern.
  3. Ignite with ferro-rod sparks into the curl core; add pencil-sized twigs after 10 seconds.
  4. Time boil of 500 ml water at 0 °C — record duration and resin mass used.

Goal: Maintain a stable flame for > 5 minutes without melt-through. Adjust base thickness and fuel spacing until achieved.

6 | Troubleshooting

  • Fire sinks into snow: Increase platform thickness or add a bark reflector underneath.
  • Sputtering flame: Resin oxidized — scrape deeper to expose fresh core.
  • Rapid soot buildup: Too little airflow; elevate fire slightly to vent gases.

7 | Safety & Sustainability

Never harvest fatwood from living pines — use fallen stumps only. Resin decomposition is slow; excessive harvesting can hinder nutrient cycling in conifer ecosystems. Store fatwood shavings in a separate, ventilated tin — volatile terpenes are flammable even at mild temperatures.

Conclusion

Firecraft in freezing conditions is chemistry, not luck. Once you understand how terpenes behave, snow and ice become variables to manage — not obstacles. Fatwood bridges biology and survival engineering: stored sunlight, distilled into heat on demand.
