Cold-Weather Firecraft & Fatwood Chemistry

Understand ignition physics and fatwood chemistry to master fire-starting when everything is frozen and wet.

Field reference only — train safely and comply with local fire restrictions.

1) Why Cold Affects Combustion

Below 0 °C, moisture within wood fibers acts as a heat sink. Every gram of water must absorb ~540 cal of latent heat to vaporize before ignition occurs. The colder and wetter your fuel, the more thermal energy your ignition source must supply. Air density also increases, changing oxygen flow dynamics within your fire lay.

2) The Chemistry of Fatwood

Fatwood forms when pine heartwood becomes saturated with resin (mostly pinene and related diterpenes). These hydrophobic hydrocarbons are energy-dense and repel water, preserving flammability. When shaved into curls, surface area increases and the volatiles vaporize easily at ~180 °C, igniting even when damp.

  • Resin ratio: 20–35 % by weight
  • Energy density: ~35 MJ/kg (comparable to diesel fuel)
  • Flash point: 180–200 °C

3) Building a Fire in Snow or Rain

  1. Platform: Lay a base of dry sticks, bark, or foil to insulate from snow melt.
  2. Tinder: Shave fatwood curls or use cotton coated in petroleum jelly.
  3. Ignition: Strike a ferro rod within 1 cm of tinder; magnesium shavings can boost temperature to >1,200 °C.
  4. Fuel stages: Pencil-thin twigs → finger-sized sticks → wrist-sized logs. Keep kindling pre-warmed under clothing if possible.

4) Field Exercise

Objective: Boil 250 mL of water at 0 °C ambient temperature.

  • Start with one ferro-rod ignition and 30 g of fatwood shavings.
  • Record time to sustained flame and to full boil.
  • Note differences when using damp vs. dry fatwood.

5) Safety & Environmental Notes

  • Never light directly under low branches or snow-loaded conifers.
  • Clear snow down to mineral soil or use a metal base plate.
  • Extinguish fully — snow can insulate embers instead of smothering them.

6) Biological Tie-In

Human thermoregulation depends on maintaining a core temperature near 37 °C. Even a 2 °C drop impairs manual dexterity and decision-making. Reliable fire sources prevent hypothermic drift by restoring caloric warmth and psychological stability. The smell of pine resin also has mild anxiolytic effects via limbic olfactory pathways — a subtle but real morale boost in survival scenarios.
